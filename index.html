Deal Desk Engine

01
live

I built a deal desk tool that takes a deal, scores it on margin and discount discipline, and either auto-approves it, routes it to the right approver, or rejects it. The decisions are deterministic, so you can trust and audit them. AI structures messy deal notes, writes the plain-English explanation, and drafts the term sheet. It never makes the decision.

The example I lead with: a deal at 78% margin still routes, because one line was discounted 35% past a per-line floor. Whether a deal makes money and whether a rep is holding the line on price are two different questions, so the tool checks both, separately.

Deals route by reason, not one yes-or-no: Net-90 terms go to Finance, a termination-for-convenience clause goes to Legal. Every decision shows its work (inputs, math, rule fired) and every threshold is config, not code, so RevOps edits a number when policy changes and the logic never moves. The decision engine calls no model, so the expensive 80% is free; three AI edges cost pennies per deal.

Built as an interview case study. Runs entirely on synthetic pricing data.

Deal Brain

02
in build · first agent shipped and measured

RevOps teams lose deals twice: once when the deal dies, again when the reason dies with it. Closed-lost dropdowns say "price" while the call transcripts say "no champion." The answer to "what's really happening on this deal?" is spread across CRM fields, field history, transcripts, emails, and Slack. Eight places to look, one question, no shared context.

Deal Brain does the plumbing once: everything syncs into one pre-joined Postgres store with a SFDC-shaped schema (including field history, the thing forecast analytics actually runs on), one get_deal_context(deal) interface, and three agents on top. A 2 to 3 hour weekly ritual becomes a 5-minute Slack brief.

Every deal is synthetic, written by a seeded script that records the intended truth as it goes: this deal has a champion, that one skipped legal review. That answer key makes accuracy measurable instead of claimed. The first of the three agents, the MEDDPICC scorecard, has been graded against it: within one level of the truth 99% of the time, overall agreement at the level you'd expect between two trained humans, about two dollars to score 100 deals. The one pillar it reads poorly is named in the repo, not buried in an average. Zero real deal data, ever.

Mandarin study app

03
on my phone via TestFlight

A personal, offline iOS study app for learning Mandarin. It replaced a three-app loop (Duolingo, a course site, a flashcard tool) with one integrated daily ritual: learn a character through a mnemonic movie-scene method, drill it with spaced repetition, then read it in graded stories with audio. Dark-first, hanzi as the hero, tone-colored pinyin, one primary action per screen.

The curriculum ships inside the app as a bundled SQLite database built by a Python pipeline: 88 lessons, an HSK 3.0 benchmark mapping, generated audio packs at normal and slow speed, and synthesized pinyin where the source drops it (347 sentences). No network dependency at runtime; the whole thing works in airplane mode.

It is also proof the infrastructure page compounds: the app was built in review-gated waves by the same Claude-plus-critique loop, then fixed from real device feedback the same way. It is what I study on now.

Cross-platform architecture

01

Content never travels machine-to-server directly. Everything meets at GitHub; live queries take a separate path over a private VPN (Tailscale). Data is tiered by sensitivity, and the tiers are walls, not conventions.

Multi-model by design. Claude builds. Every high-consequence change is critiqued by a second model family (Codex) before it ships, because blind spots are correlated: the verifier must be independent of the source. Codex also implements now: I hand it a written spec and it builds in an isolated git worktree, with the token cost of every dispatch logged. Gemini is the third engine, used for 1M-context whole-vault reads and as a tiebreaker when the first two disagree. The VPS agents run their own model family, watched by a daily liveness probe rather than a failover; the off-provider fallback existed, missed surfaces in a real outage, and got decommissioned. An alert I trust beats a fallback I don't. No vendor is an unchecked authority.

The agents + what they do

02

Four groups, and only two of them are agents. Alfred and Arnold are the user-facing ones, scoped profiles on the VPS, reachable from my phone over Telegram at any hour. The Hermes stack is the machinery behind them: an orchestrator that routes and schedules, a fenced researcher, and the pipelines they feed. Claude OS is not on the VPS at all; it is the desk-side builder that constructs and maintains everything here. Profiles are narrow by design and coordinate through a shared task board and a shared memory bank, not by talking to each other. The ingest engine

03

The capture rule: zero-structure input, automated organization. Nothing asks me to file, tag, or format at capture time. I send a link, save a post, or clip a page; the pipeline does the rest.

Untrusted web content is treated as hostile until proven otherwise. The autonomous researcher is tool-less: it can only write inert markdown. A dual-LLM firewall sanitizes and classifies every capture, a deterministic regex scan quarantines anything carrying injection markers, and a human gate sits before the wiki. I assume prompt injection will be attempted and design for it.

The wiki itself is Karpathy-style: flat markdown, no RAG, no embeddings, pages organized by function (sources, patterns, concepts, guides) with per-domain verdict pages that record what to adopt and what to skip.

Memory

04

Four stores, one rule: truth lives in files, and sensitivity decides where a store runs. Anything a model writes automatically is a disposable lens over the files, never the source of truth; the wipe-and-rebuild path is tested, not assumed.

The episodic layer is Hindsight, an open-source memory service self-hosted next to the same Postgres. It exists for the one job files lose: agents on three machines doing autonomous work with no memory of each other. At session end it extracts typed facts from the conversation (what happened, what was decided, opinions with a confidence score), stores them time-aware so stale facts retire on their own, and recalls by meaning, keyword, and recency together. The payoff is the briefing: every session opens with what the other agents did since I was last there. The desk builds something, the phone knows; Alfred decides something, the next desk session starts already caught up.

llm wikiThe knowledge store is a Karpathy-style wiki: flat markdown, no RAG, no embeddings, links resolve by filename, so it survives model and tool changes. An earlier semantic search index was built, measured, and retired once flat-file navigation proved sufficient.
deterministic recallWiki lookups run through a scoring retriever (index lines + filename tokens, no model in the loop) that opens only the top page. About 60% cheaper and 55% faster than letting the model read around.
injection defenseThe researcher, which ingests hostile web content, gets no recall at all: giving the untrusted-input surface access to shared memory is an exfiltration vector. And any memory derived from external content carries a provenance tag and surfaces as a claim with a source, never an instruction.
continuityEvery session ends in a written handoff and starts warm from it; an append-only session log is the continuity record. A cold start reads one file, not a history.

Optimizations

05

This is the unglamorous layer that keeps the system cheap and legible. Most of it is subtraction: fewer tokens loaded, fewer files opened, and the expensive model only where consequence demands it.

okf indexesEvery managed folder carries an index.md describing its contents, so an agent loads cheap folder metadata before opening files (Google's Open Knowledge Format pattern). Navigation cost drops before a single document is read.
model routingTwo operating points, nothing in between: an everyday model for routine work, escalation to the frontier the moment a trigger fires (irreversibility, trust boundaries, ambiguous requirements). Consequence beats category.
review dispatchSecond-model critiques run in an isolated subagent, so the bulky review transcript is billed once instead of riding along in every later turn (about 18% measured saving).

Security posture

06

The operating principle: capabilities are built but fenced. Every action sits behind an approval gate, a live flag, or a scoped token until I deliberately open it.

exposureThe VPS runs non-root; services publish on the private VPN address only. The sole public listener is key-only SSH. A Docker egress allowlist blocks abuse ports.
secretsKeys live only in gitignored env files, isolated per component in a container the agents cannot read, with a written rotation runbook.
trust gatesA second, independent model family critiques every hard-to-reverse change before it ships. Calendar writes take a two-phase confirm. Hooks block destructive git operations.
piiThe most sensitive tier never leaves the laptops. People's names are masked in the database. Agents holding personal data write to their own isolated memory banks, and no fallback provider exists for PII to leak to.
untrusted inputWeb content is fenced by the ingest pipeline above: tool-less researcher, dual-LLM firewall, deterministic quarantine, human gate.

ai-gateway

08

reusable infrastructure · build once, use many

A standalone browser tool can't hold an API key: anyone can read it in DevTools. The fix is one small Cloudflare Worker that fronts the Anthropic API for every static, no-backend front-end I build. The page POSTs to the gateway; the gateway holds the key server-side and forwards the call. A new tool is one config entry, and no key ever ships to a browser.

The endpoint is public, so the design assumes protection, not secrecy: four stacked guardrails cap the worst case at a known ceiling.

layerwhat it caps
1 · spend capa hard monthly limit at the provider; the backstop for everything below
2 · rate limitper-IP daily budget, so one abuser can't drain the month
3 · allowlistcheap models + capped tokens per project; expensive calls are impossible
4 · origin + tokenrotatable client token and origin check; a soft gate, not a vault

Build your own

09

I distilled the whole build into a starter kit: the build order (seven steps, each buying a specific capability), a phased VPS setup runbook with a security checklist, de-personalized templates for the context files, a secrets-101 guide, and copy-paste prompts that point your own Claude Code at each step.

It exists because three of the seven steps are where everyone gets stuck, and the kit puts a verification at the end of every risky step: prove the gitignore holds before the first push, prove the VPN wall refuses outside connections before going always-on.
